I firmly believe that enduring software in the age of computing should be a top priority, and it shouldn't be slow, have bugs, be undocumented, or be hard to fix.

The most satisfying parts of my job are:

  • Measuring a performance gain (even of a few milliseconds), even after going insane launching a program 10s of times;
  • Commiting a change that deletes a bunch of old crufty code;
  • Pouring my heart out in code comments, discussing the philosophy and *why* of the logic‚Äîwhat worked, what didn't, trade-offs and benefits, and so on;
  • Starting a new project with control in mind and minimizing dependencies;

Growing up without access to a fast computer, I found myself tinkering with low-level programming: game engines, embedded software, and eventually a kernel from scratch for the Raspberry PI.

My gurus are industry people like Yan Chernikov, Jonathan Blow, Casey Muratori, whom I follow regularly and who have taught me a large part of the craft of software engineering.

I like Casey's philosophy of seeing our modern day programmers job more as code explorers than "telling the computer what to do strictly confined by the possibility space of hardware" programmer per se. Our job should be to get our hands dirty and try many different strategies for solving problems in order to find the optimal one (or a very good one). So how do we judge that? In the wild jungle, our map and compass are data-oriented design (ultimately the most important thing at the end of the day is what goes in and out of a computer) and measurable performance metrics.
