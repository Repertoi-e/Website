Growing up without access to a fast computer, I found myself tinkering with low-level programming: game engines, embedded software, and eventually a kernel from scratch for Raspberry PI.

I see myself more as a code explorer than a programmer per se, because I tend to get dirty and try many different strategies problem in order to find the optimal one. (+ my gen z edginess going against modern programming practices). In the wild jungle, my map and compass are data-oriented design (ultimately the most important think at the end of the day is what goes in and out of a computer) and measurable performance metrics.

The most satisfying parts of my job are:
  • Measuring a performance gain (even of a few milliseconds), after going insane to launch a program 10s of times;
  • Commiting a change which deletes a bunch of old crufy code;
  • Pouring out my soul in comments, talking about the philosophy and the *why* of the code - what worked, what didn't, trade-offs/benefits, etc.;
  • Starting a new project with control in mind and minimizing dependencies;
I firmly believe that enduring software in the age of computing is a top priority, and it shouldn't be slow, buggy, undocumented or unmaintable.

My gurus are industry people like Yan Chernikov, Jonathan Blow, Casey Muratori, who are responsible for teaching me a large part of the craft of software engineering.

The last few months I started taking pictures of everyone and everything, and filming short scenes and videos. For me, the meaning of life would ultimately lie somewhere between engineering and creativity. Although I'm still searching for that, this page layout (photos combined with projects) is my take on that.
